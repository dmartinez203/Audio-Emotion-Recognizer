Final Open-Ended Neural Network Project

Introduction to Neural Networks — Senior Computer Science Project (10-Day Timeline)

1. Project Overview

This is your last project in the Introduction to Neural Networks course. The goal is to take a step beyond what we have covered in class and demonstrate that you can explore, understand, and apply a new neural network or deep learning approach independently.

You will:

  • Choose a type of neural network (or deep learning method) that was not covered in our lectures or labs.
  • Identify a realistic problem or challenge whose solution can reasonably involve that model.
  • Conduct a brief research investigation to understand the method and related prior work.
  • Implement a minimal but working prototype and evaluate it on data.
  • Summarize your work clearly in a written report and a short demo/presentation.
Important: This project is intentionally open-ended. You are not expected to build a production system. The focus is on learning something new, reasoning about its capabilities, and demonstrating a functioning proof-of-concept.

2. Core Requirements

2.1 New Architecture / Method

Your project must be centered on a type of neural network or deep learning algorithm that we have not studied in class. Examples (only if they were not previously covered) include:

  • Transformers (for text, images, or time series)
  • Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
  • Attention-based sequence models beyond standard RNNs/LSTMs
  • Autoencoders and Variational Autoencoders
  • GAN variants (e.g., StyleGAN, CycleGAN) with a specific application
  • Diffusion models for image or signal generation
  • Capsule Networks
  • Spiking Neural Networks
  • Neural ODEs or other specialized architectures

If you are unsure whether a candidate method qualifies as “not covered,” choose an approach that is clearly outside the examples and material used in class.

2.2 Problem or Challenge

You must identify a problem or challenge whose solution can reasonably be framed as a neural network or deep learning task. Examples:

  • Predicting something from structured or time-series data (e.g., anomaly detection, demand forecasting, health indicators).
  • Classifying, generating, or transforming text (e.g., summarization, style transfer, code completion).
  • Working with images or video (e.g., super-resolution, segmentation, denoising, simple generative art).
  • Recommender-style tasks (e.g., basic personalized suggestions from small datasets).
  • Small-scale scientific, game, or simulation problems that map naturally to a model you select.
Keep the scope practical: a problem that can be addressed with a small or medium dataset and a simple prototype is better than an overly ambitious topic that cannot be completed in 10 days.

2.3 Research Component

Your project must include a clear research element:

  • Read and reference at least 2–3 credible sources (research papers, surveys, or technical articles) about your chosen method.
  • Summarize what is known: strengths, limitations, and typical use cases.
  • Explain why this method is appropriate (or at least plausible) for the problem you selected.

This is not a literature review course, but you do need to demonstrate that you engaged with existing work instead of treating this as a random coding exercise.

3. Suggested Directions (Not Mandatory)

The following are ideas to spark your thinking. You are free to pursue something entirely different as long as it fits the requirements.

  • Transformers for Time-Series Forecasting: Use a simplified transformer model to predict a short time-series (stock prices, sensor data, or energy usage) and compare it against a simple baseline.
  • Graph Neural Networks for Small Networks: Model a small social network, citation network, or user–item graph and try node classification or link prediction.
  • Autoencoders for Dimensionality Reduction or Denoising: Use an autoencoder to compress and reconstruct images or tabular data, or to denoise noisy samples.
  • GAN or Diffusion for Simple Image Generation: Train a basic GAN or diffusion model on a small, well-curated set of images (e.g., handwritten digits or icons) and show what it learns.
  • Capsule Networks or Spiking Networks: Explore how these architectures handle a small classification task and report what you observe compared to standard CNNs or MLPs.
  • Neural ODEs or Physics-Informed Models: Use a neural ODE or related approach to model a very simple dynamical system (e.g., a damped oscillator) and compare to standard numerical methods.

4. 10-Day Suggested Timeline

This is a suggested schedule to keep the project manageable within 10 days. Adjust as needed, but do not leave everything for the last two days.

  1. Day 1–2: Choose your architecture and problem. Skim several sources and lock in your topic.
  2. Day 3–4: Do focused reading. Take notes on how the method works and typical implementations. Collect or prepare your dataset.
  3. Day 5–6: Implement a first working version (even if small). Get a model running end-to-end on a subset of your data.
  4. Day 7–8: Improve training and evaluation. Add basic metrics, plots, and comparisons to a simple baseline if possible.
  5. Day 9: Write the first full draft of your report and prepare your demo (notebook, script, or slides).
  6. Day 10: Polish everything. Make sure your code runs from start to finish, your report is coherent, and your demo is ready.
Aim for complete and modest rather than incomplete and ambitious. A small, well-explained experiment is enough.

5. Required Deliverables

You must submit the following:

  1. Project Report (3–6 pages or equivalent):
    • Clear description of the problem or challenge you chose.
    • Explanation of your chosen architecture/deep learning method and why it is relevant.
    • Summary of your research sources and what you learned from them.
    • Overview of your dataset and preprocessing steps.
    • Model design and training procedure (hyperparameters, loss, optimizer, etc.).
    • Results: metrics, plots, sample outputs, or qualitative evaluation.
    • Discussion of what worked, what did not, and what you would try next with more time.
  2. Code and Instructions:
    • All code needed to run your experiment (e.g., a Git repo or compressed folder).
    • A short README with:
      • Environment or library requirements (e.g., Python version, PyTorch/TensorFlow, key packages).
      • Exact steps to run your code and reproduce main results.
      • Notes on how to obtain or include the dataset.
  3. Short Demo or Presentation (5–8 minutes):
    • Briefly state the problem and chosen model.
    • Show your key results (plots, screenshots, short live run, etc.).
    • Share the single most important thing you learned from this project.

6. Advice for a Successful Project

  • Pick something narrow and concrete. A small, clearly defined problem is better than trying to “solve everything” with a fancy model.
  • Start with an existing implementation. It is acceptable to adapt open-source examples or tutorials, as long as you understand the code and clearly credit your sources. Your contribution should be in adapting, experimenting, and explaining, not just copying.
  • Treat baselines seriously. If you can, compare your advanced model with a simple baseline (e.g., logistic regression, a small MLP, or a basic non-neural method). This shows whether the extra complexity is justified.
  • Expect some frustration. New architectures often come with messy details (e.g., shapes, training stability). Keep notes on what breaks and how you fix it; this is part of what you will discuss in your report.
  • Document as you go. Don’t wait until the end to write your report. Capture decisions, errors, and insights throughout the 10 days.
  • Respect the time limit. If something is taking too long, reduce the scope: smaller dataset, fewer training epochs, or a simpler variant of the architecture.

7. What This Project Is Really Testing

This assignment is not about who can train the largest model or get the highest accuracy. It is about:

  • Your ability to explore a new neural network or deep learning idea independently.
  • Your ability to formulate and scope a problem that is appropriate for that method.
  • Your ability to connect research reading to implementation and reflect on what you observe.
  • Your ability to communicate technical work clearly, honestly, and concisely.

Treat this as a small-scale rehearsal of what real-world work and research look like: defining a problem, finding tools, experimenting, and explaining your choices.
